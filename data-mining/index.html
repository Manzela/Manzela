The Problem

No Vendor for the Job.

A small Israeli financial services firm needed targeted prospects. The available enterprise data platforms didn’t serve the market — wrong language, wrong geography, wrong price point.

list_alt

Generic Lists, Low Conversion

Sales worked from purchased lead lists with no geographic or income filtering. Conversion was poor because the prospect base wasn’t the right one — cold-calling people who had no need for wealth management or pension planning.

domain_disabled

No Vendor for This Market

Enterprise data platforms (ZoomInfo, Clearbit, Apollo) were US-focused, priced at enterprise tier, and had thin coverage of Israeli business and professional records. None of them indexed the Hebrew-language sources where the actual prospects lived.

explore_off

Geographic Targeting by Hand

Filtering prospects by income tier, neighborhood, or postal code — the dimensions that actually predicted whether someone needed financial services — required a custom layer that no off-the-shelf tool offered for this region.

target

Engineering objective. Build a custom pipeline that pulls publicly available business and professional data from the open web, geo-classifies it by income tier, scores each record for financial-services relevance, and delivers structured prospect lists to the sales team in the format they already use — replacing what would otherwise have been an enterprise data subscription that didn’t exist for this market anyway.

The Build

Five Stages from Public Web to Sales-Ready List

Each stage was a discrete unit with a single job. The pipeline ran on a workstation; outputs landed on the sales team’s shared drive in Excel.

my_location
STAGE 01

Target Definition · Geo & Income Classification

Before scraping anything, the pipeline needed to know where to look. Israeli postal codes, cities, and neighborhoods were classified into income tiers using publicly available demographic indicators, real-estate price signals, and municipal classifications. The output was a ranked geographic-target list — the high-density areas where the prospect base for wealth-management services was most likely to live. Every downstream stage filtered against this list.

How the geo-target list was built
01 · INVENTORY
Postal codes & areas
National inventory of postal codes, cities, and named neighborhoods.
02 · SIGNAL
Public price & demo data
Real-estate transaction averages and demographic indicators per area.
03 · RANK
Income-tier scoring
Areas scored by composite signal; high-tier surfaced for targeting.
04 · OUTPUT
Geo-target list
Filtered list passed downstream as the scraping scope.
Why geo first. Scraping every record on the public web and filtering at the end would have wasted months. Filtering geography first collapsed the search space by an order of magnitude before a single page was fetched.
cloud_download
STAGE 02

Extraction · Source-Tuned Scrapers

Python scrapers — BeautifulSoup for static pages, Selenium for JavaScript-rendered pages — pulled publicly available records: business registries, professional directories, public listings, and municipal records. Each scraper was tuned to a specific source’s structure, rate-limited to be polite, and ran in batches against the geo-target list from Stage 01. Raw HTML was cached locally before parsing, so a parser change didn’t require re-fetching.

storefront Business registries
badge Professional directories
apartment Public real-estate listings
account_balance Municipal records
Why source-tuned scrapers. Each source had a different DOM structure. A generic configurable scraper would have meant maintaining a config layer for every site — one-off scrapers were cheaper to write, easier to repair when a source changed its markup, and isolated failures to a single source without taking the whole pipeline down.
data_object
STAGE 03

Structuring · Normalize, Schema, Deduplicate

Raw extractions were noisy — duplicate records across sources, mixed Hebrew/English encodings, inconsistent address and name formats. A Pandas pipeline normalized records into a single schema, deduplicated on a fuzzy match across name + location + profession, and persisted the cleaned dataset to a local SQLite store. Every record in the database was guaranteed to have a known shape and a unique identity.

Unified record schema
name Normalized full name, encoding-corrected.
contact Phone or other public channel where available.
location Postal code + neighborhood + geo-tier from Stage 01.
profession Occupation or business category, mapped to a controlled vocab.
source Which scraper found the record — for traceability.
raw_notes Source-specific fragments preserved for the classifier.
Why dedupe before classify. A single prospect surfacing under three slightly different spellings across three sources would inflate volume and waste sales time on duplicate calls. The fuzzy match collapsed those into one canonical record before any scoring touched it.
tune
STAGE 04

Classification · Relevance Scoring

Each cleaned record was scored against a set of relevance signals for financial services. The output was a ranked prospect list — sorted by likelihood that a cold-call would land on someone who actually had a need for wealth management, pension planning, or insurance brokerage. This is the layer that turned data into a usable product.

SIGNAL · 01 Geo-tier (Stage 01) Income-tier of the prospect’s area — the strongest single predictor.
SIGNAL · 02 Profession weight Lawyers, doctors, business owners and senior professionals scored higher.
SIGNAL · 03 Business-ownership indicator Presence of a registered business under the same name lifted the score.
SIGNAL · 04 Cross-source coverage Records that appeared in multiple sources scored higher — signal of presence.

The scraping was the easy part.
The classifier was the product.

Tuned against feedback. Scoring weights were adjusted manually between batches based on conversion-rate signal from the sales team. Each batch came back with a sense of which leads landed and which didn’t; the next batch reflected what was learned. The classifier was the single place in the pipeline where iteration paid the most.
outbox
STAGE 05

Delivery · Excel to the Sales Team

Sales worked in Excel. The final stage exported the ranked prospect list into themed workbooks — sorted by region, profession, and score band — with all relevant fields formatted for cold outreach. Workbooks were timestamped, versioned, and dropped to a shared drive. The sales team never had to interact with the pipeline; they just opened a file.

Meet users where they are. Sales had no appetite for a dashboard, no tolerance for a login screen, and no patience for a CRM integration. Excel was the right interface — invisible engineering on the back end, the familiar tool on the front. Forcing a custom UI would have killed adoption regardless of how good the leads were.
System Architecture

A Local Pipeline, Not a Platform

No cloud, no API gateway, no monitoring stack — by design. The right amount of infrastructure for a one-engineer system serving one team.

desktop_windows

Local Compute

Python on a single workstation. Each scraper, the structuring step, the classifier, and the exporter were independently runnable scripts — no server, no daemon, no orchestrator.

database

Local Storage

SQLite for the structured record store, cached HTML for re-parsing, and Excel for the sales-facing output. No managed database. Backups were file copies.

schedule

Manual Orchestration

Cron for recurring scrapes, manual review between batches, and a deliberate human in the loop on the classifier weights. The pipeline operator was the orchestrator.

Why no cloud. The data lived on one machine, the engineer was one person, the consumer was one team in the same office. Cloud infrastructure would have added cost and operational surface without solving any constraint that existed. The architecture matched the team — that match was the point.

Reflection

What This Project Taught

The lessons from a 17-month custom pipeline outlasted the pipeline itself. They recurred, at much larger scale, in everything that came after.

01
Data quality eats data volume. Early batches pulled large volumes and converted at near-zero. Tighter geo-filtering and stricter classification thresholds reduced volume and lifted conversion. The same lesson recurred years later at twenty-thousand-record scale at Tasko.ai: the bottleneck was never the model, it was the pipeline that produced its inputs.
02
A custom pipeline beats a generic platform when the domain is narrow. Enterprise data tools generalize at the cost of the specializations that actually mattered for this market: language, geography, regulatory context, target profile. A scoped pipeline ran cheaper, ranked better, and changed faster. The same trade-off resurfaces every time the choice is between buying a horizontal platform and building a vertical one.
03
The classifier is the product. Anyone can scrape. The layer that decides whether a pipeline ships value is the one between raw data and the customer’s decision — and that layer is bought with iteration on real feedback, not engineering elegance. The first thing the team felt was the classifier, not the scrapers.

For seventeen months the sales team at Eitam Finance worked from leads generated by this pipeline instead of purchased generic lists. Cost: a workstation and an engineer. Replaced: an enterprise data subscription that wasn’t available for this market anyway.

The same pattern — extract from a noisy source, classify into something useful, deliver in the format the consumer already uses — recurred at much larger scale: a 21,000-record service taxonomy at Tasko.ai, and millions of product pages through a deterministic-gate-first DAG at the Pipeline Observatory. The earliest version of that thinking lived here.
